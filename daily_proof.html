Theorem

Theorem Let us have $m$ IID samples $S$ from an unknown distribution over the reals, if the probability of an interval $[a,b]$ is at least $\epsilon$, then: \begin{equation} m \geq \frac{1}{\epsilon} \ln \frac{1}{\delta} \rightarrow Pr\big[\exists. x \in S \cap [a,b] \big] \geq 1-\delta \end{equation} Proof. The probability of $x \not \in [a,b]$ is $1 - \epsilon$. The probability of sampling $m$ samples such that none of them are in $[a,b]$ is $(1-\epsilon)^{m}$. Probability that we have an example in $[a,b]$ is $1-(1-\epsilon)^{m}$. We want to lower bound $1-(1-\epsilon)^{m}$. Hence, \begin{align} &1-(1-\epsilon)^{m} \geq 1 - \delta\\ &-(1-\epsilon)^{m} \geq - \delta\\ &(1-\epsilon)^{m} \leq \delta\\ & m \log (1-\epsilon) \leq \log \delta\\ & m \geq \frac{\log \delta}{\log (1-\epsilon)} & \log(1-\epsilon) \text{ is negative}\\ & m \geq \frac{\log \delta}{\log (1-\epsilon)} \geq \frac{1}{-\epsilon} log \delta & \log(1+x) \leq x, \text{ for } x > -1 \\ & m \geq \frac{1}{\epsilon} {log \frac{1}{\delta}} \end{align}
